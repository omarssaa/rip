Have you ever wondered how the bonds between atoms were classified to be covalent or ionic? Well, this page will tell some information about this topic.

Electronegativity(\(\chi\)) is a measure of the ability of an atom to attract the shared electrons of the chemical bond towards itself.

Electronegativity itself depend on some factors like the atomic number and the distance between the valence shell electrons and the nucleus.

The electronegativity increases as we go from left to right through the period. Whereas it usually decreases from the top of the group to the bottom of it.

The reason why the electronegativity increases from left to right is due to the increase in atomic number from left to right while the number of electron shells remain the same, so the attraction force of electrons towards the nucleus increases and the atomic radius decreases which results in the increase of electronegativity.

On the other hand, it decreases from top to bottom because the number of electron shells increase and the shielding(screening) effect increases, so the attraction force of electrons towards the nucleus decreases and the atomic radius decreases resulting in the decrease in electronegativity.

A useful way to determine the type of a bond between two atoms is by determining the difference in electronegativity between those two atoms.

You can use two ways to determine the type of bonds according to the difference in electronegativity whether by using the table below, or by using the Van Arkel‐Ketelaar triangle.

Difference in electronegativity (\(Δ\chi\)) Type of bond
0≤(\(Δ\chi\))<0.4 Non-polar covalent bond
0.4≤(\(Δ\chi\))<1.7 Polar covalent
1.7≤(\(Δ\chi\)) Ionic
